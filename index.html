UX Copy Rewrite

Onboarding & Error States

Context: B2B project management SaaS — rewriting onboarding microcopy and error messages to reduce friction and drop-off
Before
Welcome to Stackboard!

Please fill out all required fields to complete your account setup. Fields marked with an asterisk (*) are required. If you experience any issues, contact our support team.
After
Let's get your workspace ready.

Takes about two minutes. We'll use this to personalize your dashboard and connect your team.
What changed: Removed compliance language ("required fields," asterisks) from the emotional first impression. The original protects the product; the rewrite serves the user. Forward momentum ("Let's") replaces procedural instruction.

Context: Mid-size SaaS analytics platform — auditing 6 high-traffic help articles for clarity, accuracy, and user alignment
Ubuntu Official Documentation — Upgrade Docs & Landing Page

This audit examines the Ubuntu documentation landing page and upgrade documentation — a high-traffic entry point for users making consequential decisions about their systems. Five findings across three categories: content currency, plain language clarity, and information architecture.

  • Broken PDF links on the documentation landing page — immediate trust erosion at the entry point
  • Last-edited timestamps revealing years of neglect, including a 2019 date on upgrade-critical content
  • Upgrade instructions referencing end-of-life versions as current examples — actively misleading for users on current releases
  • Writing that assumes a technical baseline mismatched to the likely audience
  • Page naming that reflects internal taxonomy rather than user intent

Research Brief

User Mental Models in Onboarding

Context: Internal brief for a product team exploring why new users abandon setup flows before completing workspace configuration
Research Brief — Onboarding Drop-off: Patterns & Signals
Prepared for
Product & Content Teams
Scope
Literature review + UX pattern analysis
Output
Strategic content recommendations

Background

Users who abandon onboarding flows before completing workspace setup represent a recoverable loss — the intent was there; the friction won. This brief synthesizes UX research on onboarding mental models and identifies content-side interventions most likely to reduce drop-off without requiring engineering resources.

Key patterns identified

  • Progress ambiguity: Users don't know how many steps remain. Without a visible endpoint, the perceived cost of continuing is undefined — and undefined cost defaults to "too high."
  • Commitment anxiety: Setup questions that feel permanent (team name, billing, integrations) trigger hesitation. Users want to explore before they commit. Copy that signals reversibility ("You can always change this later") measurably reduces drop-off.
  • Value deferred: Users abandon when they can't see what they're configuring toward. Showing a preview of the finished dashboard state during setup addresses this directly.
  • Terminology friction: When onboarding uses product-internal terms ("workspace," "environment," "instance") before users have context for them, confusion compounds. Plain language at the entry point; product vocabulary earned over time.

Content-side recommendations

  • Add a persistent step counter (Step 2 of 4) with time estimates at each stage
  • Rewrite commitment-heavy prompts to foreground reversibility
  • Replace product jargon in first three onboarding screens with plain-language equivalents
  • Add a single-sentence "why we ask" annotation beneath each data collection field
"Users don't abandon onboarding because your product is bad. They abandon because the cost of continuing feels uncertain and the value of finishing feels abstract."

Style Guide Excerpt

Voice & Tone Guidelines

Context: Excerpt from a content style guide for a B2B SaaS product — covering brand voice, tone calibration by context, and common usage rules
Brand Voice — Who We Sound Like
Direct, not blunt.
We say what we mean in the fewest words that still feel human. We don't pad. We also don't bark.
Confident, not smug.
We know our product works. We don't need to oversell it or perform expertise. We show, don't tell.
Helpful, not hand-holdy.
We respect that our users are smart. We give them what they need and get out of the way.
Human, not quirky.
Warmth comes from clarity and care, not from puns. We don't do "whimsy." We do real.
Tone by Context
Context Tone Example
Onboarding Warm, forward-moving "Let's get your workspace ready — takes about two minutes."
Error states Calm, specific, actionable "Couldn't connect. Check that your API key is active, then try again."
Empty states Inviting, low-pressure "Nothing here yet. Add your first project to get started."
Success states Brief, genuine "Done. Your report is ready to share."
Billing / sensitive Clear, neutral, no spin "Your plan renews on March 14. You'll be charged $49."
Common Usage Rules

Contractions

We'll get that sorted out for you.
We will get that sorted out for you.

Use contractions in UI copy and conversational contexts. Avoid in legal, billing, or highly technical documentation.

Button labels

Save changes / Connect Slack / Export report
Submit / Confirm / OK / Proceed

Labels should name the action, not describe the gesture. "Save changes" tells users what happens. "Submit" tells them nothing.

Referring to users

You can update this anytime.
Users can update this anytime. / The user may update this.

Address the user directly. Third-person creates distance and reads like documentation written for an audience that isn't in
